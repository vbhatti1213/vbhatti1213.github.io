Strategic Positioning

AFRA doesn't talk about mental health as a crisis resource. It positions mental health as professional standard of care.

Most first responder mental health communications lead with trauma statistics and crisis intervention language. That approach activates shame, not behavior change. AFRA's brand strategy flipped the frame — normalizing peer support as part of the job, not an admission of failure. The positioning anchors on the idea that psychological fitness is the same as physical fitness. It's not weakness to train. It's how you stay on the job.

The Brief

The core tension

First responders are trained to push through. Asking for help runs against the grain of professional identity. Communications that lead with vulnerability don't land with this audience — they confirm the stigma.

The data behind it

792 psychological injury WCB claims filed in Alberta in 2021. 227 denied. The system wasn't built for these workers. AFRA's communications had to address the gap — not as advocates, but as peers.

Communications Strategy
01

Peer voice first

Institutional voice creates distance. Peer voice creates permission. Every campaign leads with the voices of first responders — not clinicians, not executives. Someone like you, saying it's okay.

02

Frame before you ask

Don't lead with asks or resources. Lead with reframing. The audience has to believe the premise before they'll accept the offering. Every post earns the CTA rather than opening with it.

03

Stay in the culture

Language matters more here than in almost any other sector. Copy uses operational vocabulary and respects the stoicism — it doesn't fight it. Strength framing, not vulnerability framing.
